HACK THE COAST 2026
Inventory Management & Order Fulfillment Optimization
Problem Background
Prince of Peace (PoP) operates 3 distribution centers (DCs) across the U.S. and ships to retailers across 9 channels. At the company level, inventory often looks healthy: a product might show 3 months of supply on hand. But that aggregate number hides dangerous site-level imbalances.
Real example: all 3 months of a product's inventory is concentrated at one DC on the East Coast, while the other two DCs are empty. When a West Coast customer orders, PoP must split-ship from two locations. The retailer receives a partial shipment and issues a short-ship chargeback. The rest arrives late, triggering a late-delivery penalty. Combined penalties: $300+ on $100 of product.
PoP's annual chargeback exposure across all cause codes is approximately $700,000. Shipment and delivery penalties specifically are in the low six figures.
In this challenge, students will build an inventory balancing and fulfillment optimization system that:
	•	Identifies site-level inventory imbalances before they cause penalties
	•	Supports the transfer-vs-wait decision (move stock between DCs or wait for inbound supply)
	•	Reduces chargeback exposure from split shipments, short ships, and late deliveries
	•	Protects supplier OTIF (On Time, In Full) scores
No prior supply chain experience is required.

Problem Statement
Design and build a system that helps PoP's operations team see and act on inventory imbalances across 3 DCs before they cascade into penalties, using real (anonymized) inventory, sales, and chargeback data.
The system should demonstrate clear improvement over the current manual, reactive process.

What the System Must Do (Requirements)
Imbalance Detection
	•	Compare inventory levels across all 3 DCs for each SKU
	•	Flag SKUs where company-wide inventory looks adequate but one or more sites are critically low or empty
	•	Rank imbalances by risk: consider demand at the understocked site, customer delivery windows, and penalty exposure
Transfer vs. Wait Decision Support
	•	When an imbalance is detected, help the ops manager decide:
	•	Transfer stock from an overstocked DC now (faster, but costs freight)
	•	Wait for an inbound shipment to arrive (cheaper, but risks stockout and penalties)
	•	Factor in: inter-DC freight costs, inbound PO expected arrival dates, known delays or FDA holds, and the cost of potential chargebacks if stock runs out
	•	Present the tradeoff clearly: cost of transfer vs. risk of penalty
Chargeback Analysis
	•	Analyze chargeback data by cause code to identify patterns:
	•	Which cause codes drive the most cost?
	•	Which customers or channels generate the most penalties?
	•	Are chargebacks concentrated in specific SKUs, DCs, or time periods?
	•	Use chargeback patterns to prioritize which imbalances to fix first
	•	Note: chargeback data includes both operational penalties (short ship, late delivery, damage) and planned promotional deductions (TPRs). Filter or flag accordingly.
Early Warning & Alerting
	•	Generate proactive alerts when a SKU at a specific DC is approaching a critical threshold
	•	Don't wait for a customer order to expose the problem
	•	Show projected days until stockout based on recent demand rate
Output & Usability
	•	Present imbalances, alerts, and transfer recommendations in a format an operations manager can act on
	•	Make the cost tradeoffs visible and clear
	•	Show improvement: compare what would have happened under the current manual process vs. the system's recommendations

Suggested Approach
Break the problem into components:
	•	Component 1 - Inventory Visibility: Load current inventory snapshot and sales history. Calculate demand rate by SKU by DC. Project days-of-supply at each site. Flag imbalances where company-wide inventory is adequate but site-level is critical.
	•	Component 2 - Inbound Supply Awareness: Load open PO data. Map expected arrivals to DCs. Identify any flagged delays. This tells the system whether relief is coming.
	•	Component 3 - Transfer Cost Model: Using freight cost estimates, model the cost of transferring stock between DCs. Compare against the expected cost of chargebacks if the imbalance is not corrected.
	•	Component 4 - Chargeback Pattern Analysis: Analyze historical chargebacks by cause code, customer, channel, and DC. Identify which imbalances are most likely to result in penalties.
	•	Component 5 - Alert & Recommendation Engine: Combine all of the above into a dashboard or alert system that surfaces the highest-priority imbalances with clear recommended actions.

Note: PoP does not have historical monthly inventory snapshots. Only a current-day snapshot is available. You can estimate past inventory levels by working backward from current inventory using historical sales and PO receipt data.

Team Collaboration Model
Teams should divide responsibilities, for example:
Data & Pipeline
	•	CSV ingestion (inventory, sales, POs, chargebacks, freight costs, warehouse profiles)
	•	Data joining and cleaning
	•	Demand rate and days-of-supply calculations
Analytics & Logic
	•	Imbalance detection and risk ranking
	•	Transfer cost vs. chargeback cost comparison model
	•	Chargeback pattern analysis
	•	Alert threshold logic
Frontend & Presentation
	•	Dashboard showing inventory health by SKU by DC
	•	Transfer recommendation interface with cost tradeoffs
	•	Before/after comparison (manual vs. system-assisted)

Suggested 2-Day Plan
Day 1
	•	Load and explore data (inventory snapshot, sales, POs, chargebacks, warehouse profiles, freight costs)
	•	Calculate demand rate and days-of-supply by SKU by DC
	•	Build imbalance detection logic
	•	Start chargeback pattern analysis
Day 2
	•	Build transfer cost model using freight estimates
	•	Compare transfer cost vs. chargeback risk for flagged imbalances
	•	Generate alerts and transfer recommendations
	•	Build demo-ready dashboard
	•	Prepare presentation with before/after scenario

Deliverables
Each team should present:
	•	How the system detects inventory imbalances across 3 DCs
	•	Transfer vs. wait decision logic and cost tradeoff model
	•	Chargeback pattern analysis: which causes, customers, or DCs drive the most penalty cost
	•	Example scenario: walk through a specific imbalance and show the system's recommendation vs. what would happen under the current manual process
	•	Key assumptions, limitations, and lessons learned

Presentation time: 6-8 minutes
